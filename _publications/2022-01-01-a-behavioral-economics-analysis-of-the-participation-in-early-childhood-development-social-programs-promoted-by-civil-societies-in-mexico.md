This article uses behavioral economics to understand why families do or do not participate in early childhood development programs promoted by civil society organizations in Mexico. Rather than assuming that low participation is simply a matter of lack of interest, the paper considers how information, perceptions, incentives, habits, and practical barriers shape decisions. That perspective is useful because many social programs underperform not because they are ineffective, but because the target population never fully engages with them. For a general audience, the study helps explain how small frictions can reduce participation in programs that could benefit children. The findings point to actionable ways to improve enrollment and retention, including better communication, more convenient access, and program design that responds to how families actually make decisions.
