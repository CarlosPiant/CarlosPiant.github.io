This paper compares therapeutic options for schizophrenia through a cost-effectiveness lens, asking which interventions produce the greatest health benefit for the resources invested. Written for decision makers working under tight budgets, the study brings together evidence on treatment costs and outcomes to show that not all clinically useful interventions offer the same value when scaled in public systems. The practical contribution is that it translates a complex psychiatric care problem into choices that ministries of health and service planners can act on. The main message is that prioritization matters: some interventions provide better value for money and can help expand access more efficiently, while less efficient alternatives may consume scarce funds without generating comparable improvements in health and functioning.
