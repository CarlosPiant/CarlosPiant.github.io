This article reviews the state of costing research for HIV interventions in sub-Saharan Africa and asks whether the available evidence is strong enough for policy decisions. Rather than focusing on one intervention, it looks across the literature to assess what has been studied, how costs were measured, and where major evidence gaps remain. The paper shows that costing work has grown, but methods and reporting practices are often inconsistent, which limits comparability across settings and programs. For non-specialists, the message is important: even well-funded health programs can make poor decisions if the underlying cost evidence is weak or incomplete. The study argues for more standardized, transparent costing methods so that future analyses can better inform budgeting, scale-up decisions, and evaluations of HIV program efficiency.
