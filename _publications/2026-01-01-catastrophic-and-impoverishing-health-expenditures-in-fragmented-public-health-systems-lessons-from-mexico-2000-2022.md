This paper examines how often households in Mexico experienced catastrophic or impoverishing health spending between 2000 and 2022, with special attention to the role of a fragmented public health system. The study asks a simple but important question: when people seek care, are they protected from financial hardship, or does the system leave some groups more exposed than others? By following long-run trends, the paper shows how fragmentation in coverage and financing can weaken financial protection and contribute to persistent inequality. For a general audience, the main finding is that health system design matters not only for access to services, but also for whether families are pushed into economic distress by out-of-pocket payments. The results support stronger, more integrated protection mechanisms in public coverage.
