This paper pools evidence from 13 countries to estimate the average costs of two important HIV services: testing and counseling, and voluntary medical male circumcision. The problem it tackles is that program costs differ widely across studies, making it hard for planners to know what numbers to trust when setting budgets or comparing interventions. By synthesizing data across settings, the study provides clearer cost benchmarks and also highlights where variation remains substantial. For a general audience, the value of the work is practical: HIV programs need realistic cost estimates to expand services efficiently. The paper shows that average costs can be summarized across countries, but it also cautions that local delivery models still matter. Better costing evidence leads to better budgeting, planning, and priority setting.
