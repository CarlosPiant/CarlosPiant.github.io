This paper studies how maternal and child health coverage improves when governments act on both sides of the problem: service supply and household demand. Supply-side efforts can expand staff, drugs, and facility readiness, while demand-side measures can reduce barriers such as cost, information gaps, or weak incentives to seek care. The paper's central contribution is showing that these strategies can reinforce one another rather than work as isolated policy tools. In plain terms, families benefit more when services are available and usable at the same time. The findings suggest that combining interventions is more likely to improve effective coverage than relying on a single policy lever. That makes the paper especially relevant for health systems trying to move beyond nominal enrollment toward real, measurable use of quality care.
