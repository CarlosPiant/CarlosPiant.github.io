This article analyzes how health financing inequality evolved in Mexico from 2000 to 2023 in the context of a fragmented health system. The central question is whether public financing has been distributed fairly across populations with different entitlements and institutional coverage arrangements. By examining long-term patterns, the paper shows that fragmentation is not only an administrative issue; it has measurable consequences for how resources are allocated and who benefits from public spending. For a general audience, the message is that unequal financing can translate into unequal opportunities to obtain timely, adequate care. The findings suggest that persistent segmentation in the system has helped sustain financing disparities over time, reinforcing the case for reforms that pool resources more equitably and reduce institutional divides.
