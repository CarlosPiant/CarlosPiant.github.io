This study looks at how efficiently outpatient maternal health services are produced across Mexico and how those patterns change across place and time. Rather than treating all providers or regions as equivalent, it identifies geographic clusters where services appear to operate more efficiently and other areas where resources may be used less effectively. The analysis then links those patterns to structural factors that may help explain why some places perform better than others. For non-specialists, the core idea is simple: the same amount of money or infrastructure does not always produce the same level of service. The findings suggest that inefficiency is not randomly distributed, and that targeted management, investment, and organizational reforms may be more useful than one-size-fits-all policies for improving maternal health service performance.
