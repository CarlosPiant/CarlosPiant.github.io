This study examines the relationship between children's nutritional status and early neurological development in socially deprived Mexican settings. The paper focuses on a period of life when nutrition and stimulation are especially important because developmental setbacks can accumulate quickly and become harder to reverse later on. By linking growth and nutrition indicators with developmental outcomes, the study helps clarify how material deprivation can affect children's early life trajectories. For a general audience, the message is straightforward: child development is shaped not only by schooling or parenting programs, but also by whether children receive adequate nutrition in the first place. The findings support integrated policies that combine nutrition, health, and early childhood development services rather than treating those areas as separate problems.
