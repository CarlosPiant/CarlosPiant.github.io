This study compiles and analyzes evidence on the cost of tuberculosis treatment in low- and middle-income countries through a systematic review and meta-regression. Its goal is not only to summarize typical treatment costs, but also to understand why those costs differ across studies and settings. The paper identifies important sources of variation, such as differences in service delivery, country context, and study design. For a general audience, the contribution is clear: tuberculosis programs need realistic cost benchmarks to plan expansion, estimate financing needs, and judge whether new strategies are affordable. The findings show that treatment costs can vary substantially, but that systematic evidence synthesis can still produce useful planning ranges. That makes the paper valuable for budgeting, program design, and future economic evaluations of TB control.
