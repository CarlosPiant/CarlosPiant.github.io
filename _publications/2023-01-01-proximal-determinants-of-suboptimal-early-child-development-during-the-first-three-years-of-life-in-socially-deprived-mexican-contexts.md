This paper investigates which proximal factors are associated with suboptimal early child development during the first three years of life in socially deprived Mexican contexts. The focus on proximal determinants is important because these are the conditions closest to the child, such as the home environment, caregiving practices, nutrition, and other immediate influences that may be more modifiable than broader structural factors. For non-specialists, the paper helps answer a practical question: where should programs intervene first if they want to improve developmental outcomes early in life? The findings highlight specific risks linked to poorer development and suggest that prevention efforts should begin early, target the household environment, and combine social, nutritional, and developmental support rather than relying on a single intervention.
