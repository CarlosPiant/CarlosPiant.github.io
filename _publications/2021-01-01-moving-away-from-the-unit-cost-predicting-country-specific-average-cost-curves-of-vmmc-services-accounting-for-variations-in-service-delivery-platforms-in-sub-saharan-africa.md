This paper argues that the cost of voluntary medical male circumcision should not be treated as a single fixed number. Instead, it models country-specific cost curves that change with service volume and delivery platform, reflecting the reality that costs can rise or fall as programs scale and operate in different ways. For a general audience, the contribution is practical: budgeting for a large prevention program is more accurate when planners know how costs behave as the program expands, rather than relying on one average cost from one study. The findings show that delivery platforms matter and that scale can change average cost substantially. This helps policy makers compare implementation strategies more realistically and avoid planning errors based on oversimplified unit-cost assumptions.
