This article examines whether the way maternal health services are financed, organized, and delivered in Mexico is aligned with the goals of universal health coverage. Instead of assuming that more coverage automatically means better access, the paper looks at how incentives, purchasing arrangements, and provider behavior shape what women actually receive. The study shows that older structural problems remained in place even as policy goals shifted toward universality. In practice, that means fragmentation, weak coordination, and misaligned incentives can limit timely, equitable maternal care. The broader contribution is policy-oriented: it argues that expanding nominal coverage is not enough if the underlying health care market still produces gaps in quality, access, and continuity, especially for women facing social and geographic disadvantage.
