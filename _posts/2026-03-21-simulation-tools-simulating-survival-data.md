<p>This chapter creates a synthetic dataset in which patients are followed after hospital discharge until readmission or censoring. The aim is to build a time-to-event outcome from a known proportional-hazards data-generating process and then check whether a fitted survival model recovers the true hazard ratios. The design is motivated by the same survival-modeling logic that underlies much of applied health-outcomes research, where time to recurrence, readmission, death, or treatment discontinuation is more informative than a simple yes-or-no indicator <span class="citation" data-cites="cox1972">Cox (<a href="#ref-cox1972" role="doc-biblioref">1972</a>)</span>; <span class="citation" data-cites="kaplan1958">Kaplan and Meier (<a href="#ref-kaplan1958" role="doc-biblioref">1958</a>)</span>.</p>
<p>The practical lesson is that survival data are not just event indicators with a clock attached. They are built from hazards, event times, and censoring times. A good simulation should make those ingredients explicit.</p>
<section id="what-variables-will-be-created" class="level2" data-number="54.1">
<h2 data-number="54.1" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="what-variables-will-be-created"><span class="header-section-number">54.1</span> What variables will be created</h2>
<p>The synthetic sample will contain the following variables.</p>
<p><code>age</code> will represent age in years. <code>chronic</code> will count chronic conditions. <code>program</code> will indicate enrollment in a transitional-care program. <code>event_time</code> will represent the latent time to readmission. <code>censor_time</code> will represent the time at which follow-up ends for administrative or other non-event reasons. <code>time</code> will be the observed follow-up time, equal to the smaller of the event and censoring times. <code>event</code> will equal 1 if readmission is observed and 0 if the observation is censored.</p>
<p>These variables recreate the basic structure of a survival dataset while remaining simple enough for the hazard logic to stay visible.</p>
</section>
<section id="the-data-generating-process" class="level2" data-number="54.2">
<h2 data-number="54.2" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-data-generating-process"><span class="header-section-number">54.2</span> The data-generating process</h2>
<p>The simulation assumes an exponential proportional-hazards model. Patient <code>i</code> has hazard</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
h_i(t) = \lambda_0 \exp(\eta_i),
\]</span></p>
<p>where</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\eta_i =
0.018(\text{age}_i - 65) +
0.30 \text{chronic}_i -
0.45 \text{program}_i.
\]</span></p>
<p>The baseline hazard is set to</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\lambda_0 = 0.0025.
\]</span></p>
<p>Under an exponential proportional-hazards model, the event time can be generated by inverse transform:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
T_i = -\frac{\log(U_i)}{\lambda_0 \exp(\eta_i)},
\]</span></p>
<p>where <span class="math inline">\(U_i \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)\)</span>. Censoring times are then generated independently, and the observed data are</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\tilde{T}_i = \min(T_i, C_i), \qquad
\Delta_i = \mathbb{1}(T_i \le C_i).
\]</span></p>
</section>

<section id="main-assumptions-behind-this-simulation" class="level2" data-number="54.7">
<h2 data-number="54.7" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="main-assumptions-behind-this-simulation"><span class="header-section-number">54.7</span> Main assumptions behind this simulation</h2>
<p>The main assumption is proportional hazards. In this synthetic design, the treatment program and the patient characteristics shift the hazard multiplicatively and that effect does not change over time. The simulation also assumes independent censoring, which means the censoring process does not depend on the unobserved event time once the simulated variables are given.</p>
<p>Because the event times were generated from an exponential baseline hazard, the true model is slightly more specific than the Cox model used for estimation. That is acceptable here because the Cox model is still correctly specified for the relative hazard structure and therefore should recover the covariate effects well.</p>

<section id="further-reading" class="level2" data-number="54.8">
<h2 data-number="54.8" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="further-reading"><span class="header-section-number">54.8</span> Further reading</h2>
<p>Cox introduced the proportional-hazards model that still anchors much of applied survival analysis <span class="citation" data-cites="cox1972">Cox (<a href="#ref-cox1972" role="doc-biblioref">1972</a>)</span>. Kaplan and Meier provide the classic nonparametric survival estimator that underlies most descriptive survival work <span class="citation" data-cites="kaplan1958">Kaplan and Meier (<a href="#ref-kaplan1958" role="doc-biblioref">1958</a>)</span>. Together, they form a strong conceptual bridge between simulated event times and the models used on real health-outcomes data.</p>
