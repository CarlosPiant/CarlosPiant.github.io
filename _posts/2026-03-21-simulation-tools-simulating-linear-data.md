<p>This chapter creates a synthetic dataset in which the outcome is continuous and generated by a linear model with additive Normal noise. The design is motivated by blood-pressure treatment studies such as SPRINT, where a continuous clinical measurement is observed after treatment and analyzed in relation to baseline risk factors and treatment assignment <span class="citation" data-cites="sprint2015">SPRINT Research Group (<a href="#ref-sprint2015" role="doc-biblioref">2015</a>)</span>. The synthetic version here is not a replication of SPRINT. Instead, it creates a simplified follow-up systolic blood pressure outcome so that the full data-generating process is known in advance. That makes it useful for checking whether an ordinary least squares regression can recover the true coefficients.</p>
<p>The practical reason to simulate linear-model data is straightforward. Continuous outcomes appear across health economics and decision sciences: systolic blood pressure, quality-of-life scores, biomarker levels, total costs after transformation, recovery time, and resource use summaries. If the conditional mean is approximately linear and the residual variation is additive, the linear model is often the first analytic tool to test. Simulation makes that structure explicit.</p>
<section id="what-variables-will-be-created" class="level2" data-number="47.1">
<h2 data-number="47.1" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="what-variables-will-be-created"><span class="header-section-number">47.1</span> What variables will be created</h2>
<p>The synthetic sample will represent patients followed for 6-month systolic blood pressure after treatment intensification. <code>baseline_sbp</code> will measure baseline systolic blood pressure in mmHg. <code>age</code> will represent age in years. <code>chronic</code> will count the number of chronic conditions. <code>intensive_treatment</code> will indicate whether the patient was assigned to a more intensive management strategy. <code>adherence</code> will indicate whether the patient remained adherent to the assigned regimen. The outcome <code>followup_sbp</code> will measure systolic blood pressure after 6 months.</p>
<p>These variables give the linear model something realistic to work with: a continuous baseline predictor, a treatment indicator, a comorbidity burden, and a behavioral factor that shifts the follow-up mean.</p>
</section>
<section id="the-data-generating-process" class="level2" data-number="47.2">
<h2 data-number="47.2" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-data-generating-process"><span class="header-section-number">47.2</span> The data-generating process</h2>
<p>The core assumption is that the continuous outcome follows</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
Y_i = \beta_0 +
\beta_1 \text{baseline\_sbp}_i +
\beta_2 \text{intensive\_treatment}_i +
\beta_3 \text{age}_i +
\beta_4 \text{chronic}_i +
\beta_5 \text{adherence}_i +
\varepsilon_i,
\]</span></p>
<p>where the residual term is Normally distributed:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2).
\]</span></p>
<p>For this simulation, the true parameters are set to</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\beta_0 = 35,\;
\beta_1 = 0.62,\;
\beta_2 = -7.5,\;
\beta_3 = 0.18,\;
\beta_4 = 1.6,\;
\beta_5 = -4.5,
\]</span></p>
<p>with</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\sigma = 8.
\]</span></p>
<p>These values imply a clinically sensible pattern. Higher baseline blood pressure predicts higher follow-up blood pressure. Intensive treatment lowers follow-up systolic pressure. Older age and more chronic conditions raise it slightly. Better adherence lowers it further.</p>
</section>

<section id="main-assumptions-behind-this-simulation" class="level2" data-number="47.8">
<h2 data-number="47.8" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="main-assumptions-behind-this-simulation"><span class="header-section-number">47.8</span> Main assumptions behind this simulation</h2>
<p>The most important assumption is linearity in the conditional mean:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\mathbb{E}(Y_i \mid X_i) = X_i \beta.
\]</span></p>
<p>The second assumption is homoskedastic Gaussian noise:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2).
\]</span></p>
<p>The third is conditional independence across observations. Those assumptions are useful for learning because they create a clean benchmark in which ordinary least squares is correctly specified.</p>
<p>Real data are often more complicated. Effects may be nonlinear, residual variance may increase with baseline risk, treatment assignment may be confounded, and outcomes may be censored or bounded. That is exactly why simulation is valuable. It gives a controlled environment before those complications are added.</p>
</section>
<section id="how-to-adapt-this-template" class="level2" data-number="47.9">
<h2 data-number="47.9" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="how-to-adapt-this-template"><span class="header-section-number">47.9</span> How to adapt this template</h2>
<p>Once the basic structure is clear, the same template can be modified easily. You can add interactions, create nonlinear baseline effects, introduce heteroskedasticity, correlate treatment assignment with baseline risk, or add clustered errors. Each change creates a new synthetic laboratory for studying how a fitted linear model behaves under a different truth.</p>
<p>In practice, this is often the most useful way to learn what ordinary least squares is actually doing. How biased does the treatment coefficient become when an important covariate is omitted? How much sample size is needed before a small treatment effect is recovered reliably? What happens when the true relationship is quadratic but the fitted model is linear? Synthetic data can answer those questions directly.</p>
</section>

<p>The linear-model framework remains a central entry point for applied regression analysis, and blood-pressure trials such as SPRINT provide a useful substantive anchor for continuous clinical outcomes <span class="citation" data-cites="sprint2015">SPRINT Research Group (<a href="#ref-sprint2015" role="doc-biblioref">2015</a>)</span>. A natural next step after this chapter is to extend the simulation by adding heteroskedasticity, treatment-effect heterogeneity, or clustering and then studying how coefficient recovery changes under misspecification.</p>
