<section id="introduction-when-your-data-remembers-the-past" class="level1">
<h1>1. Introduction: when your data remembers the past</h1>
<p>Most of the models we've seen so far assume that observations are <strong>independent</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>One patient's cost doesn't depend on another's,</li>
<li>One person's utility score doesn't depend on someone else's.</li>
</ul>
<p>But in <strong>time series</strong>, your data absolutely remembers the past:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monthly hospital admissions this month look a lot like last month,</li>
<li>Daily ED visits are higher on Mondays (because... Mondays),</li>
<li>Quarterly spending tends to follow trends and cycles.</li>
</ul>
<p>If you try to ignore this dependence and just throw everything into a simple regression, your residuals will quietly revolt and become <strong>autocorrelated</strong>.</p>
<p>This is where <strong>ARMA models</strong> step in:</p>
<ul>
<li>AR = <strong>Autoregressive</strong>: today depends on <strong>past values</strong> of the series.</li>
<li>MA = <strong>Moving Average</strong>: today depends on <strong>past shocks</strong> (errors).</li>
<li>ARMA = a combo of both: your series is influenced by what it <em>used to be</em> and the shocks it <em>used to receive</em>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In this tutorial we will:</p>
<ul>
<li>Introduce AR, MA, and ARMA models (gently, with math),</li>
<li>Fit an ARMA model in R (using synthetic data you can map to HEOR concepts),</li>
<li>Check diagnostics and interpret the model,</li>
<li>Discuss why ARMA-type models are useful in <strong>HEOR and health policy</strong>,</li>
<li>Provide a few classic references if you want to go deeper.</li>
</ul>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="foundations-ar-ma-and-arma-in-plain-language" class="level1">
<h1>2. Foundations: AR, MA, and ARMA in plain language</h1>
<p>We'll consider a time series <span class="math inline">\(Y_t\)</span> observed at equally spaced times (e.g., months, quarters, years).</p>
<p>Think of <span class="math inline">\(Y_t\)</span> as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monthly hospital admissions,</li>
<li>Monthly average cost per patient,</li>
<li>Quarterly mortality rates, etc.</li>
</ul>
<section id="autoregressive-ar-models" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="autoregressive-ar-models">2.1. Autoregressive (AR) models</h2>
<p>An <strong>autoregressive model of order <span class="math inline">\(p\)</span></strong>, written AR(<span class="math inline">\(p\)</span>), says:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>Today's value is a linear combination of <strong>previous values</strong> of the series, plus some noise.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Formally:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
Y_t = \phi_1 Y_{t-1} + \phi_2 Y_{t-2} + \cdots + \phi_p Y_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t,
\]</span></p>
<p>where:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\phi_1, \dots, \phi_p\)</span> are autoregressive coefficients,</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\varepsilon_t\)</span> is white noise (mean zero, constant variance, uncorrelated).</li>
</ul>
<p>Example intuition:</p>
<ul>
<li>AR(1): <span class="math inline">\(Y_t\)</span> depends on <span class="math inline">\(Y_{t-1}\)</span>.<br>
If <span class="math inline">\(\phi_1\)</span> is close to 1, the series is <strong>highly persistent</strong>.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="moving-average-ma-models" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="moving-average-ma-models">2.2. Moving Average (MA) models</h2>
<p>A <strong>moving average model of order <span class="math inline">\(q\)</span></strong>, written MA(<span class="math inline">\(q\)</span>), says:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>Today's value depends on <strong>current and past shocks</strong> (errors), not on past values of <span class="math inline">\(Y_t\)</span> directly.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Formally:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
Y_t = \mu + \varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \theta_2 \varepsilon_{t-2} + \cdots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q},
\]</span></p>
<p>where:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\mu\)</span> is the mean (or intercept),</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\theta_1, \dots, \theta_q\)</span> are moving average coefficients,</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\varepsilon_t\)</span> is white noise.</li>
</ul>
<p>Intuition:</p>
<ul>
<li>MA(1): today's value is influenced by today's shock and last period's shock.</li>
<li>Shocks have <strong>short memory</strong>: after a few periods, their impact fades.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="arma-models-combining-ar-and-ma" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="arma-models-combining-ar-and-ma">2.3. ARMA models: combining AR and MA</h2>
<p>An <strong>ARMA(<span class="math inline">\(p, q\)</span>)</strong> model combines both AR and MA parts:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
Y_t = \phi_1 Y_{t-1} + \cdots + \phi_p Y_{t-p}
  + \varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \cdots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q}.
\]</span></p>
<p>Key ideas:</p>
<ul>
<li>The AR part captures <strong>dependence on past levels</strong>.</li>
<li>The MA part captures <strong>dependence on past shocks</strong>.</li>
<li>Together, they can represent a wide variety of <strong>stationary</strong> time series patterns.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="stationarity-the-dont-forget-this-condition" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="stationarity-the-dont-forget-this-condition">2.4. Stationarity (the "don't forget this" condition)</h2>
<p>ARMA models assume that the time series is <strong>stationary</strong>, roughly meaning:</p>
<ul>
<li>The mean does not change over time,</li>
<li>The variance does not change over time,</li>
<li>The correlation between <span class="math inline">\(Y_t\)</span> and <span class="math inline">\(Y_{t-k}\)</span> depends only on the lag <span class="math inline">\(k\)</span>, not on <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In practice:</p>
<ul>
<li>A strong trend or strong seasonal pattern usually violates stationarity.</li>
<li>To handle those, we often difference the series (leading to ARIMA models).<br>
But in this tutorial, we'll stick to an ARMA situation where stationarity is plausible.</li>
</ul>

<section id="why-arma-models-matter-in-heor-and-health-policy" class="level1">
<h1>6. Why ARMA models matter in HEOR and health policy</h1>
<p>You might be thinking:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>"Cool math, but where does this help me justify a policy or publish a paper?"</p>
</blockquote>
<p>ARMA and related time series models are very useful in HEOR and health policy.</p>
<section id="modeling-and-forecasting-utilization-and-costs" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="modeling-and-forecasting-utilization-and-costs">6.1. Modeling and forecasting utilization and costs</h2>
<p>Many HEOR questions care about <strong>how things evolve over time</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monthly hospital admissions,</li>
<li>Quarterly ED visits,</li>
<li>Annual costs per patient,</li>
<li>Drug utilization over time.</li>
</ul>
<p>ARMA models can:</p>
<ul>
<li>Capture short-term dynamics and persistence,</li>
<li>Provide forecasts with <strong>uncertainty intervals</strong>,</li>
<li>Help plan <strong>capacity</strong>, <strong>budgeting</strong>, and <strong>resource allocation</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Even simple ARMA forecasts can inform:</p>
<ul>
<li>"How many admissions should we expect next winter?"</li>
<li>"What is the likely range of costs given past patterns?"</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="baselines-for-policy-evaluation" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="baselines-for-policy-evaluation">6.2. Baselines for policy evaluation</h2>
<p>When evaluating a <strong>policy change</strong> (e.g., new screening guidelines, payment reforms), it helps to know:</p>
<ul>
<li>What would the outcome have looked like <strong>without</strong> the policy?</li>
</ul>
<p>ARMA or ARIMA models can be used to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fit a time series model to pre-policy data,</li>
<li>Forecast the "counterfactual" time series,</li>
<li>Compare observed data after the policy to the forecasted baseline.</li>
</ul>
<p>This provides a <strong>structured and transparent</strong> baseline for impacts on:</p>
<ul>
<li>Utilization,</li>
<li>Costs,</li>
<li>Outcomes (e.g., mortality, admissions).</li>
</ul>
</section>

<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="understanding-dynamics-not-just-levels">6.3. Understanding dynamics, not just levels</h2>
<p>A lot of HEOR focuses on cross-sectional averages, but time series models let us ask:</p>
<ul>
<li>How quickly do effects fade or persist over time?</li>
<li>Do shocks (e.g., a pandemic wave, a supply disruption) have short-lived or long-lived impacts?</li>
<li>Are there recurring patterns (e.g., seasonal peaks)?</li>
</ul>
<p>ARMA models are often the first step in:</p>
<ul>
<li>Characterizing <strong>dynamic patterns</strong>,</li>
<li>Building more complex time series structures,</li>
<li>Combining with regression (e.g., ARIMAX models with covariates).</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="foundations-for-more-advanced-time-series-methods" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="foundations-for-more-advanced-time-series-methods">6.4. Foundations for more advanced time series methods</h2>
<p>Understanding ARMA models gives you a base to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Move to ARIMA (with differencing),</li>
<li>Add seasonal components (SARIMA),</li>
<li>Explore state-space models, Kalman filters,</li>
<li>Use Bayesian time series methods.</li>
</ul>
<p>In HEOR, where datasets often include <strong>longitudinal aggregates</strong> (e.g., monthly hospital data over many years), this toolkit is highly relevant.</p>

<section id="further-reading" class="level1">
<h1>7. Further reading</h1>
<p>If you'd like to go deeper into ARMA and time series methods (with plenty of R examples), here are four solid sources:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><p><strong>Shumway &amp; Stoffer - <em>Time Series Analysis and Its Applications</em></strong><br>
Accessible treatment of time series with a good mix of theory and applications, including ARMA/ARIMA models.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Hyndman &amp; Athanasopoulos - <em>Forecasting: Principles and Practice</em> (online, free)</strong><br>
Very practical, R-focused introduction (using the <code>forecast</code> and <code>fable</code> ecosystems). Great for applied forecasting.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Box, Jenkins, Reinsel, and Ljung - <em>Time Series Analysis: Forecasting and Control</em></strong><br>
The classic "Box-Jenkins" text. More technical, but the foundational reference for ARIMA-style modeling.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Chatfield - <em>The Analysis of Time Series: An Introduction</em></strong><br>
Introductory but thorough, with a strong focus on understanding the data and practical modeling considerations.</p></li>
</ol>
<p>Armed with these, you can go from "I once heard of ARMA" to "I routinely fit ARIMA models and argue with them in meetings" - which is, arguably, a core HEOR skill. 😄</p>
