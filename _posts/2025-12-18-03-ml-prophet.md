<section id="introduction-when-you-want-a-friendly-time-series-model" class="level1">
<h1>1. Introduction: when you want a friendly time-series model</h1>
<p>By now you've met:</p>
<ul>
<li>ARIMA: the classic, slightly formal time series friend,</li>
<li>ARMA: the "I know all your autocorrelations" cousin.</li>
</ul>
<p>Enter <strong>Prophet</strong>, which is like the colleague who says:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>"Don't worry, I'll handle the trend, seasonality, and holidays for you - just give me a data frame."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Prophet was designed with a "data scientist first" mindset:</p>
<ul>
<li>You give it a time series with a date column,</li>
<li>It decomposes the series into <strong>trend + seasonality + holidays</strong>,</li>
<li>It produces a forecast with intervals and nice plots,</li>
<li>You don't have to wrestle with ACF/PACF unless you want to.</li>
</ul>
<p>Is it magic? No.&nbsp;Is it convenient and often quite good? Yes.</p>
<p>In this tutorial we'll:</p>
<ul>
<li>Lay out the main ideas behind Prophet,</li>
<li>Fit a Prophet model in R on synthetic "hospital admissions" data,</li>
<li>Compare Prophet with ARIMA in terms of assumptions, workflow, and flexibility,</li>
<li>List 4 strengths and 4 limitations,</li>
<li>Explain why Prophet-style models matter for <strong>HEOR and health policy</strong>,</li>
<li>Point to some further reading (including critical perspectives).</li>
</ul>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="foundations-what-is-prophet-doing" class="level1">
<h1>2. Foundations: what is Prophet doing?</h1>
<p>Prophet models a time series <span class="math inline">\(y(t)\)</span> as a sum of components:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
y(t) = g(t) + s(t) + h(t) + \varepsilon_t,
\]</span></p>
<p>where:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="math inline">\(g(t)\)</span> is a <strong>trend</strong> term,</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(s(t)\)</span> captures <strong>seasonality</strong> (weekly, yearly, etc.),</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(h(t)\)</span> captures <strong>holiday / special event effects</strong>,</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\varepsilon_t\)</span> is an error term.</li>
</ul>
<section id="trend" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="trend">2.1. Trend</h2>
<p>The trend <span class="math inline">\(g(t)\)</span> is typically modeled as a piecewise linear or logistic growth curve, with:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Change points</strong> where the slope can shift,</li>
<li>Priors that control how flexible the trend is.</li>
</ul>
<p>This is helpful when:</p>
<ul>
<li>There are changes in growth rate (e.g., policy introduction, new program),</li>
<li>The series doesn't follow a simple straight or gently curved line.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="seasonality" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="seasonality">2.2. Seasonality</h2>
<p>Prophet uses <strong>Fourier series</strong> to model seasonality:</p>
<ul>
<li>Annual seasonality (e.g., winter peaks),</li>
<li>Weekly seasonality (e.g., Monday vs Sunday),</li>
<li>Additional seasonalities (e.g., monthly, if needed).</li>
</ul>
<p>These are smooth periodic components added to the model.</p>
</section>
<section id="holidays-and-special-events" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="holidays-and-special-events">2.3. Holidays and special events</h2>
<p>You can pass a data frame with <strong>holiday dates</strong> (and possibly regions), and Prophet will estimate:</p>
<ul>
<li>Effects for those dates,</li>
<li>Potentially spillover effects for days around them.</li>
</ul>
<p>For HEOR/health policy, "holidays" could also represent:</p>
<ul>
<li>Special campaigns,</li>
<li>Shocks,</li>
<li>Implementation dates for big reforms,</li>
<li>Pandemics (though those are more than just "holidays" 😅).</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="prophet-as-a-generalized-additive-model-gam" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="prophet-as-a-generalized-additive-model-gam">2.4. Prophet as a generalized additive model (GAM)</h2>
<p>Under the hood, Prophet is a kind of <strong>additive model</strong> with:</p>
<ul>
<li>Parametric trend,</li>
<li>Smooth seasonal components,</li>
<li>Optional holiday regressors.</li>
</ul>
<p>You can think of it as:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>A GAM that's pre-configured to be extremely convenient for common forecasting tasks, especially daily or monthly data.</p>
</blockquote>
<hr>
</section>
</section>

<section id="strengths-and-limitations-of-prophet" class="level1">
<h1>4. Strengths and limitations of Prophet</h1>
<section id="four-strengths" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="four-strengths">4.1. Four strengths</h2>
<ol type="1">
<li><p><strong>Very user-friendly interface</strong><br>
You give it <code>ds</code> and <code>y</code>, get a model and forecast with trend + seasonality + holidays, and a plot. Great for quick applied work and exploratory forecasting.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Handles trend changes and seasonality well</strong><br>
Prophet is particularly good when:</p>
<ul>
<li>There are clear seasonal patterns (yearly, weekly),</li>
<li>Trends change at a few points,</li>
<li>You have daily or monthly granular data.</li>
</ul></li>
<li><p><strong>Built-in holiday / special event effects</strong><br>
You can easily specify "holiday" dates (or pseudo-holidays like major policy launches) and let the model estimate their effects.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Interpretable decomposition</strong><br>
Prophet's decomposition into trend, seasonality, and holidays is easy to visualize and explain to non-technical stakeholders, which is valuable in policy and HEOR settings.</p></li>
</ol>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="four-limitations" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="four-limitations">4.2. Four limitations</h2>
<ol type="1">
<li><p><strong>Less flexible for complex autocorrelation structures</strong><br>
Prophet focuses on trend + seasonality + holidays, but does not explicitly model short-range autocorrelation the way ARMA does. Residual autocorrelation can remain.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Defaults can be misleading if not checked</strong><br>
Because Prophet is so "automatic," it can be tempting to accept defaults. But:</p>
<ul>
<li>Trend priors might be too flexible or too rigid,</li>
<li>Seasonal components might underfit or overfit,</li>
<li>Diagnostics are still needed.</li>
</ul></li>
<li><p><strong>Not always the best for short series or weak seasonality</strong><br>
When there is:</p>
<ul>
<li>Very little data,</li>
<li>No clear seasonality,</li>
<li>Unstable patterns,</li>
</ul>
<p>simpler models (or ARIMA) can perform as well or better, with less complexity.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Model assumptions may not match all HEOR use cases</strong><br>
Prophet's structure is tuned for typical business/website time series (daily counts, clear seasonal patterns). Some HEOR data (e.g., irregular measurements, strong interventions, structural breaks) may require more custom modeling.</p></li>
</ol>
<hr>
</section>
</section>
<section id="prophet-vs-arima-a-quick-comparison" class="level1">
<h1>5. Prophet vs ARIMA: a quick comparison</h1>
<p>Here's a rough qualitative comparison to help you decide when each might shine.</p>
<section id="modeling-philosophy" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="modeling-philosophy">5.1. Modeling philosophy</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>ARIMA</strong>:
<ul>
<li>Focuses on <strong>autoregression, differencing, and moving averages</strong>.</li>
<li>Often assumes stationarity after differencing.</li>
<li>Captures short-term dynamics very explicitly.</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong>Prophet</strong>:
<ul>
<li>Treats the series as <strong>trend + seasonality + holidays</strong>.</li>
<li>Uses change points and smooth seasonal terms.</li>
<li>Emphasizes interpretable decomposition and easy configuration.</li>
</ul></li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="practical-workflow" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="practical-workflow">5.2. Practical workflow</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>ARIMA</strong>:
<ul>
<li>You may look at ACF/PACF, try different orders, check residuals.</li>
<li><code>auto.arima()</code> helps, but some understanding of ARIMA structure is helpful.</li>
<li>Seasonality is handled via SARIMA terms.</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong>Prophet</strong>:
<ul>
<li>You pass a data frame with <code>ds</code> and <code>y</code>.</li>
<li>You optionally add holidays/extra regressors.</li>
<li>You rely heavily on defaults, with tuning via priors and seasonalities.</li>
</ul></li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="data-patterns" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="data-patterns">5.3. Data patterns</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>ARIMA</strong> tends to work well when:
<ul>
<li>The series is reasonably stationary after transformations,</li>
<li>Dynamics are dominated by short memory and simple seasonality,</li>
<li>You have a moderate length of history.</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong>Prophet</strong> tends to work well when:
<ul>
<li>There is a strong, smooth trend,</li>
<li>Clear, recurring seasonal patterns (e.g., yearly, weekly),</li>
<li>Potentially important holidays or events.</li>
</ul></li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="communication-and-interpretation" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="communication-and-interpretation">5.4. Communication and interpretation</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>ARIMA</strong>:
<ul>
<li>Well-known in statistical time series literature,</li>
<li>Parameters have interpretation in terms of lags and shocks,</li>
<li>Less visually decomposed by default.</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong>Prophet</strong>:
<ul>
<li>Decomposition plots (trend, yearly seasonality, weekly seasonality, holidays) are very intuitive,</li>
<li>Easier to explain to non-technical audiences: "this is seasonal pattern over the year, here's the long-term trend, here are holiday bumps."</li>
</ul></li>
</ul>
<p>In practice, an HEOR analyst might:</p>
<ul>
<li>Use ARIMA and Prophet in parallel,</li>
<li>Compare forecasts via rolling-origin validation,</li>
<li>Choose based on performance + interpretability + ease of use.</li>
</ul>

<section id="why-prophet-style-models-matter-for-heor-and-health-policy" class="level1">
<h1>6. Why Prophet-style models matter for HEOR and health policy</h1>
<p>Prophet (and similar additive time series models) can be especially helpful in HEOR and policy work because they:</p>
<section id="provide-quick-interpretable-forecasts" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="provide-quick-interpretable-forecasts">6.1. Provide quick, interpretable forecasts</h2>
<p>For questions like:</p>
<ul>
<li>"What will monthly admissions look like over the next 2-3 years?"</li>
<li>"What's the likely range of drug utilization if seasonal patterns continue?"</li>
</ul>
<p>Prophet provides:</p>
<ul>
<li>Forecasts with intervals,</li>
<li>Trend and seasonality plots,</li>
<li>Fast turnaround for scenario discussions.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="highlight-seasonal-and-holiday-effects" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="highlight-seasonal-and-holiday-effects">6.2. Highlight seasonal and holiday effects</h2>
<p>Seasonality and special events matter for:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Capacity planning</strong> (e.g., winter peaks, holiday dips),</li>
<li><strong>Staffing</strong>,</li>
<li><strong>Supply chain management</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Prophet's decomposition makes it easy to show:</p>
<ul>
<li>"Here are winter peaks,"</li>
<li>"Here is the effect of holidays,"</li>
<li>"Here is the underlying trend."</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="serve-as-baselines-for-policy-evaluation" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="serve-as-baselines-for-policy-evaluation">6.3. Serve as baselines for policy evaluation</h2>
<p>You can use Prophet to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Model pre-policy trends and expected seasonal patterns,</li>
<li>Forecast a <strong>counterfactual</strong> trajectory (what would have happened without the policy),</li>
<li>Compare observed post-policy series to the forecasted baseline.</li>
</ul>
<p>While not a substitute for a full causal design (DiD, ITS with more structure, etc.), it can provide:</p>
<ul>
<li>A transparent baseline,</li>
<li>A way to communicate expected vs observed trajectories.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="integrate-into-broader-decision-models" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="integrate-into-broader-decision-models">6.4. Integrate into broader decision models</h2>
<p>Prophet forecasts (and seasonal decompositions) can feed into:</p>
<ul>
<li>Budget impact models,</li>
<li>Capacity and resource planning tools,</li>
<li>Simulation models that require time-varying inputs.</li>
</ul>
<p>Because Prophet returns a full distribution of forecasts, you can also:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sample from forecast distributions,</li>
<li>Propagate uncertainty into downstream models.</li>
</ul>
