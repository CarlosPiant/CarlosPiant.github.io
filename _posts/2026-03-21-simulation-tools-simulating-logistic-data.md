<p>This chapter creates a synthetic dataset for a binary infertility outcome generated by a logistic model. The design is inspired by the reproductive-history application studied by Trichopoulos and coauthors, who examined how prior spontaneous and induced abortions related to secondary infertility <span class="citation" data-cites="trichopoulos1976">Trichopoulos et al. (<a href="#ref-trichopoulos1976" role="doc-biblioref">1976</a>)</span>. The synthetic version here is not a replication of their retrospective case-control design. Instead, it creates a prospective-style binary-outcome dataset so that the full logistic data-generating process is known in advance. That makes it useful for testing whether a fitted logistic regression can recover the true parameters.</p>
<p>The purpose of the chapter is straightforward. We want to build data in a way that is transparent, reproducible, and mathematically interpretable. The same logic can later be adapted for hospital readmission, policy uptake, treatment adherence, mortality, screening participation, or any other binary outcome that appears across health economics and decision sciences.</p>
<section id="what-variables-will-be-created" class="level2" data-number="51.1">
<h2 data-number="51.1" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="what-variables-will-be-created"><span class="header-section-number">51.1</span> What variables will be created</h2>
<p>The synthetic sample will contain the following variables.</p>
<p><code>age</code> will represent age in years. <code>parity</code> will count prior births. <code>education</code> will have three levels: low, medium, and high. <code>spontaneous</code> will count prior spontaneous abortions in grouped form as 0, 1, or 2, where 2 means two or more. <code>induced</code> will do the same for prior induced abortions. <code>smoking</code> will be a binary risk factor. The outcome <code>infertility</code> will equal 1 if infertility occurs and 0 otherwise.</p>
<p>These variables are chosen because they give the logistic model something realistic to work with: a mix of continuous, binary, ordinal, and categorical predictors, all of which are common in applied health data.</p>
</section>
<section id="the-data-generating-process" class="level2" data-number="51.2">
<h2 data-number="51.2" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-data-generating-process"><span class="header-section-number">51.2</span> The data-generating process</h2>
<p>The core assumption is that the outcome follows a Bernoulli distribution with success probability determined by a logit link:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
Y_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p_i),
\]</span></p>
<p>with</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\text{logit}(p_i) =
\beta_0 +
\beta_1 \text{age}_i +
\beta_2 \text{parity}_i +
\beta_3 \mathbb{1}(\text{education}_i = \text{medium}) +
\beta_4 \mathbb{1}(\text{education}_i = \text{high}) +
\beta_5 \text{spontaneous}_i +
\beta_6 \text{induced}_i +
\beta_7 \text{smoking}_i.
\]</span></p>
<p>For this simulation, the true coefficients are set to</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\beta_0 = -2.3,\;
\beta_1 = 0.045,\;
\beta_2 = -0.32,\;
\beta_3 = -0.28,\;
\beta_4 = -0.46,\;
\beta_5 = 1.05,\;
\beta_6 = 0.78,\;
\beta_7 = 0.42.
\]</span></p>
<p>These choices imply a substantively reasonable pattern. Older age raises risk. Higher parity lowers risk. Higher education is somewhat protective relative to the low-education group. Prior spontaneous and induced abortions increase risk, and smoking adds an additional increase.</p>
<p>The simulation uses a large enough sample that the fitted model should recover the true parameters reasonably well, though not perfectly. That last phrase matters. A simulation is still a sample, so the estimates should be close to the truth on average, not identical in every run.</p>
</section>

<p>That code has done two conceptually different things. First, it created the covariates. Second, it used the logistic formula to map those covariates into probabilities, and then drew a binary outcome from a Bernoulli distribution. The probabilities are the systematic part of the model. The Bernoulli draw is the stochastic part.</p>

<section id="main-assumptions-behind-this-simulation" class="level2" data-number="51.7">
<h2 data-number="51.7" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="main-assumptions-behind-this-simulation"><span class="header-section-number">51.7</span> Main assumptions behind this simulation</h2>
<p>This synthetic dataset is only as informative as the assumptions built into it.</p>
<p>The first assumption is that the logit is the correct link. The second is that the relationship between the covariates and the log-odds is linear in the chosen coding. The third is that the observations are conditionally independent. The fourth is that the included variables are enough to describe the data-generating process, since here we are simulating without omitted confounding or measurement error.</p>
<p>Those assumptions are useful for learning, but real data are often messier. Real outcomes may have clustering, nonlinear effects, misclassification, selection bias, or missing variables. That is why simulation is valuable. It gives a clean benchmark before those complications are introduced.</p>
</section>
<section id="how-to-adapt-this-template" class="level2" data-number="51.8">
<h2 data-number="51.8" class="anchored" data-anchor-id="how-to-adapt-this-template"><span class="header-section-number">51.8</span> How to adapt this template</h2>
<p>Once the basic structure is clear, the same simulation template can be modified easily. You can change the coefficient sizes, alter the event rate by moving the intercept, add interactions, add nonlinear terms, create treatment assignment mechanisms, or introduce measurement error and missingness. Each of those changes gives a new synthetic laboratory for testing methods.</p>
<p>In practice, simulation becomes especially useful when you want to know how an estimator behaves under a known truth. Does separation create instability? How many observations are needed before a coefficient is recovered reliably? What happens when a continuous covariate is nonlinear but the fitted model is not? These are exactly the kinds of questions synthetic data can answer.</p>
</section>
