<section id="introduction-when-before-and-after-is-not-enough" class="level1">
<h1>1. Introduction: when "before and after" is not enough</h1>
<p>Suppose a new health policy is rolled out:</p>
<ul>
<li>A new screening guideline,</li>
<li>A change in copayments,</li>
<li>A hospital payment reform.</li>
</ul>
<p>You look at the outcome <strong>before</strong> and <strong>after</strong> the policy and see that things improved.<br>
Victory! Policy works! ...right?</p>
<p>Not so fast.</p>
<p>During the same period:</p>
<ul>
<li>The economy might have changed,</li>
<li>Clinical practice might have improved everywhere,</li>
<li>A pandemic might have happened (hi, 2020).</li>
</ul>
<p>Comparing only <strong>before vs after</strong> in the treated group mixes up:</p>
<ul>
<li>The effect of the policy, <strong>and</strong></li>
<li>Everything else that changed over time.</li>
</ul>
<p>Difference-in-differences (DiD) says:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>"Let's bring in a <strong>comparison group</strong> that didn't get the policy and see how <em>their</em> outcome changed over the same period."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Then we:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li>Look at the change over time in the <strong>treated</strong> group.</li>
<li>Look at the change over time in the <strong>comparison</strong> group.</li>
<li>Subtract the two.</li>
</ol>
<p>That double difference (the "difference in differences") is our estimate of the policy's effect - under some important assumptions.</p>
<p>In this tutorial we will:</p>
<ul>
<li>Lay out the basic DiD setup and assumptions,</li>
<li>Show how to implement a simple DiD in R with synthetic data,</li>
<li>List some key <strong>strengths</strong> and <strong>limitations</strong>,</li>
<li>Explain why DiD matters a lot in <strong>HEOR and health policy</strong>,</li>
<li>Point to good resources if you want to dig deeper.</li>
</ul>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="foundations-the-basic-did-setup" class="level1">
<h1>2. Foundations: the basic DiD setup</h1>
<p>We start with the classic <strong>two-by-two</strong> setup:</p>
<ul>
<li>Two time periods: <strong>before</strong> (pre) and <strong>after</strong> (post) a policy.</li>
<li>Two groups:
<ul>
<li>A <strong>treated</strong> group (exposed to the policy in the post period),</li>
<li>A <strong>comparison</strong> (or control) group (not exposed).</li>
</ul></li>
</ul>
<p>Let:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="math inline">\(Y_{it}\)</span> be the outcome for unit <span class="math inline">\(i\)</span> at time <span class="math inline">\(t\)</span>,</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(D_i\)</span> be 1 if unit <span class="math inline">\(i\)</span> is in the treated group, 0 if in the comparison group,</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\text{Post}_t\)</span> be 1 if we are in the post period, 0 in the pre period.</li>
</ul>
<p>The simple DiD regression model is:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
Y_{it} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 D_i + \beta_2 \text{Post}_t
+ \beta_3 (D_i \times \text{Post}_t) + \varepsilon_{it}.
\]</span></p>
<p>Interpretation:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\beta_0\)</span> is the <strong>average outcome</strong> in the comparison group, pre period.</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\beta_1\)</span> is the <strong>baseline difference</strong> between treated and comparison, pre period.</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\beta_2\)</span> is the <strong>common time trend</strong> affecting both groups.</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\beta_3\)</span> is the <strong>DiD estimator</strong>: the treatment effect (under assumptions).</li>
</ul>
<p>In words:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>DiD effect = (Change over time in treated group) - (Change over time in comparison group).</p>
</blockquote>
<hr>
<section id="the-did-table-view" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-did-table-view">2.1. The DiD "table" view</h2>
<p>You can also think of DiD as working with four group means:</p>
<ul>
<li>Treated, pre: <span class="math inline">\(\bar{Y}^{\text{T, pre}}\)</span></li>
<li>Treated, post: <span class="math inline">\(\bar{Y}^{\text{T, post}}\)</span></li>
<li>Comparison, pre: <span class="math inline">\(\bar{Y}^{\text{C, pre}}\)</span></li>
<li>Comparison, post: <span class="math inline">\(\bar{Y}^{\text{C, post}}\)</span></li>
</ul>
<p>Then:</p>
<ul>
<li>Change in treated group: <span class="math inline">\(\bar{Y}^{\text{T, post}} - \bar{Y}^{\text{T, pre}}\)</span></li>
<li>Change in comparison group: <span class="math inline">\(\bar{Y}^{\text{C, post}} - \bar{Y}^{\text{C, pre}}\)</span></li>
</ul>
<p>The DiD estimator is:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\text{DiD} =
\left( \bar{Y}^{\text{T, post}} - \bar{Y}^{\text{T, pre}} \right)
- \left( \bar{Y}^{\text{C, post}} - \bar{Y}^{\text{C, pre}} \right).
\]</span></p>
<p>In the simple regression above, <span class="math inline">\(\hat{\beta}_3\)</span> is equal to this DiD estimate.</p>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="the-key-assumption-parallel-trends" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-key-assumption-parallel-trends">2.2. The key assumption: parallel trends</h2>
<p>The heart of DiD is the <strong>parallel trends assumption</strong>:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>In the absence of treatment, the treated and comparison groups would have experienced the <strong>same change over time</strong> in the outcome.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In other words:</p>
<ul>
<li>The comparison group tells us what would have happened to the <strong>treated group</strong> if they had <strong>not</strong> received the policy.</li>
<li>We do <strong>not</strong> require that the levels are the same pre-treatment, but we do require that the <strong>trend</strong> is comparable.</li>
</ul>
<p>If this assumption fails (for example, the treated group was already on a different trajectory for reasons unrelated to the policy), DiD can be biased.</p>
<p>This is why pre-treatment data (multiple periods before the policy) are very helpful for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Checking whether trends looked similar pre-policy,</li>
<li>Strengthening the case for parallel trends.</li>
</ul>

<section id="strengths-and-limitations-of-did" class="level1">
<h1>4. Strengths and limitations of DiD</h1>
<section id="four-key-strengths" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="four-key-strengths">4.1. Four key strengths</h2>
<ol type="1">
<li><p><strong>Controls for time-invariant unobserved differences</strong><br>
Any <strong>fixed</strong> difference between groups (e.g., underlying risk, baseline infrastructure, stable culture) is differenced out, as long as it does not change over time differently by group.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Simple and intuitive</strong><br>
The basic DiD estimator has a clear table-based interpretation and a simple regression form. It is easy to explain to clinicians, policymakers, and other stakeholders.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Uses natural experiments and policies</strong><br>
DiD works well when assignment is driven by <strong>policies or external rules</strong> (not individual choice), such as changes in reimbursement, eligibility thresholds, or rollouts across regions.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Flexible extension</strong><br>
The basic idea can be extended to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Multiple time periods,</li>
<li>Unit fixed effects and time fixed effects,</li>
<li>Covariates,</li>
<li>Staggered treatment timing (with modern estimators).</li>
</ul></li>
</ol>
</section>
<section id="four-key-limitations" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="four-key-limitations">4.2. Four key limitations</h2>
<ol type="1">
<li><p><strong>Parallel trends assumption is untestable and critical</strong><br>
DiD relies on the idea that, without treatment, treated and comparison groups would have changed in parallel. We can <strong>inspect</strong> pre-trends, but we can never fully prove this assumption.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Time-varying confounders can bias estimates</strong><br>
If something else (besides the treatment) changes over time <strong>differently across groups</strong> and affects the outcome, DiD will generally attribute that difference to the treatment.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Composition changes and migration issues</strong><br>
If the composition of the treated or comparison group changes over time (e.g., different types of patients, hospitals entering or exiting), DiD can be misleading unless these changes are properly addressed.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Complications with staggered adoption and dynamic effects</strong><br>
In settings where units adopt treatment at different times, the <strong>two-way fixed effects</strong> DiD estimator can mix different comparisons in problematic ways. Modern methods (e.g., cohort-specific estimators, event-study approaches) are needed, and the simple DiD intuition needs refinement.</p></li>
</ol>

</section>
</section>
<section id="why-did-matters-in-heor-and-health-policy" class="level1">
<h1>5. Why DiD matters in HEOR and health policy</h1>
<p>Difference-in-differences is one of the <strong>workhorse designs</strong> for evaluating health policies and interventions in the real world.</p>
<p>Some reasons why it is especially important in HEOR and health policy:</p>
<section id="evaluating-policy-changes-and-reforms" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="evaluating-policy-changes-and-reforms">5.1. Evaluating policy changes and reforms</h2>
<p>Many questions in HEOR ask:</p>
<ul>
<li>What was the effect of a new <strong>reimbursement policy</strong>?</li>
<li>Did a new <strong>screening guideline</strong> change outcomes?</li>
<li>How did a <strong>pay-for-performance</strong> scheme impact quality or costs?</li>
</ul>
<p>These changes are often rolled out:</p>
<ul>
<li>At specific times,</li>
<li>To certain regions, hospitals, or patient groups, but not others.</li>
</ul>
<p>DiD uses these differences to construct a <strong>quasi-experimental comparison</strong> and estimate causal effects.</p>
</section>
<section id="leveraging-routinely-collected-data" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="leveraging-routinely-collected-data">5.2. Leveraging routinely collected data</h2>
<p>HEOR frequently uses:</p>
<ul>
<li>Claims data,</li>
<li>Electronic health records,</li>
<li>Registries,</li>
<li>Surveys and administrative datasets.</li>
</ul>
<p>These data often have:</p>
<ul>
<li>Clear time stamps,</li>
<li>Information on which individuals or regions were affected by a policy.</li>
</ul>
<p>DiD gives a natural way to exploit this <strong>rich longitudinal structure</strong> without needing randomization.</p>
</section>
<section id="informing-economic-evaluations-and-decision-models" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="informing-economic-evaluations-and-decision-models">5.3. Informing economic evaluations and decision models</h2>
<p>Policy-relevant models often need:</p>
<ul>
<li>Estimates of <strong>effectiveness</strong> (e.g., change in utilization, health outcomes),</li>
<li>Sometimes by subgroup, region, or implementation context.</li>
</ul>
<p>DiD studies can provide <strong>policy-relevant causal estimates</strong> that feed into:</p>
<ul>
<li>Cost-effectiveness analyses,</li>
<li>Budget impact models,</li>
<li>Health technology assessments.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="communicating-results-to-policymakers" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="communicating-results-to-policymakers">5.4. Communicating results to policymakers</h2>
<p>DiD's structure maps nicely onto the way policy people think:</p>
<ul>
<li>"What changed after the policy where it was implemented?"</li>
<li>"What changed in similar places without the policy?"</li>
<li>"What is the difference between those changes?"</li>
</ul>
<p>This makes it easier to turn technical results into <strong>narratives</strong> and <strong>graphs</strong> that are comprehensible and persuasive in policy discussions.</p>

</section>

<section id="further-reading" class="level1">
<h1>6. Further reading</h1>
<p>If you want to strengthen your DiD skills (especially for applied work), here are four very useful references:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><p><strong>Angrist &amp; Pischke - <em>Mostly Harmless Econometrics</em></strong><br>
The DiD chapter is a modern classic: clear, intuitive, and focused on practical issues.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Wing, Simon, and Bello-Gomez (2018). <em>Designing Difference in Difference Studies for Public Health Policy Evaluation.</em></strong><br>
A very accessible paper focusing on DiD in public health, including graphical diagnostics and design issues.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Goodman-Bacon (2021). <em>Difference-in-Differences with Variation in Treatment Timing.</em></strong><br>
Explains the issues with two-way fixed-effects DiD when treatment timing is staggered and introduces a helpful decomposition.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Roth et al.&nbsp;(2023). <em>What's Trending in Difference-in-Differences? A Synthesis of the Recent Econometrics Literature.</em></strong><br>
A more advanced overview of recent methodological developments in DiD, useful for staying current in applied research.</p></li>
</ol>
<p>With DiD in your toolkit, you are better equipped to tackle the classic HEOR question:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>"Given that we cannot randomize this policy, how can we still say something credible about what it did?"</p>
</blockquote>
