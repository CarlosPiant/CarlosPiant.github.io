<section id="introduction-when-your-regression-is-a-bit-too-excited" class="level1">
<h1>1. Introduction: when your regression is a bit too excited</h1>
<p>Ordinary least squares (OLS) regression is like an over-caffeinated student:</p>
<ul>
<li>It tries <strong>very hard</strong> to fit the data,</li>
<li>It may chase every little wiggle,</li>
<li>Give it many correlated predictors and it can go completely off the rails.</li>
</ul>
<p>Enter <strong>ridge regression</strong>, which is basically OLS with a seatbelt:</p>
<blockquote class="blockquote">
<p>"You can still fit the data, but I'm going to gently punish you if your coefficients get too big."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Ridge regression adds a <strong>penalty on the size of the coefficients</strong>. The idea:</p>
<ul>
<li>Large coefficients = more "complex" model,</li>
<li>Complexity is risky when you have noisy or collinear predictors,</li>
<li>So we shrink coefficients toward zero to reduce variance and improve prediction.</li>
</ul>
<p>In this tutorial we'll:</p>
<ul>
<li>Explain the math behind ridge regression,</li>
<li>Fit a ridge model in R using <code>glmnet</code>,</li>
<li>Compare it informally to OLS,</li>
<li>Discuss why ridge matters for <strong>HEOR and health policy</strong>, especially with many correlated variables,</li>
<li>Share some references for deeper dives.</li>
</ul>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="foundations-what-is-ridge-regression" class="level1">
<h1>2. Foundations: what is ridge regression?</h1>
<section id="the-classic-linear-regression-setup" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="the-classic-linear-regression-setup">2.1. The classic linear regression setup</h2>
<p>Suppose we have:</p>
<ul>
<li>Outcome <span class="math inline">\(y \in \mathbb{R}^n\)</span>,</li>
<li>Predictors <span class="math inline">\(X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}\)</span> (columns are features),</li>
<li>Coefficients <span class="math inline">\(\beta \in \mathbb{R}^p\)</span>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In OLS, we estimate <span class="math inline">\(\beta\)</span> by minimizing:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\min_{\beta} \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \beta)^2
\quad = \quad \min_{\beta} \|y - X\beta\|_2^2.
\]</span></p>
</section>
<section id="ridge-regression-objective" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="ridge-regression-objective">2.2. Ridge regression objective</h2>
<p>Ridge regression modifies this by adding an <span class="math inline">\(L_2\)</span> penalty:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\min_{\beta} \; \|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\beta\|_2^2
= \min_{\beta} \; \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \beta)^2 + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p \beta_j^2.
\]</span></p>
<p>Where:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="math inline">\(\lambda \ge 0\)</span> is the <strong>regularization parameter</strong>,</li>
<li>Larger <span class="math inline">\(\lambda\)</span> → stronger penalty → <strong>more shrinkage</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Key properties:</p>
<ul>
<li>When <span class="math inline">\(\lambda = 0\)</span>, we get OLS,</li>
<li>As <span class="math inline">\(\lambda \to \infty\)</span>, coefficients shrink toward zero,</li>
<li>Ridge never sets coefficients <strong>exactly</strong> to zero (no variable selection),</li>
<li>It stabilizes estimation in the presence of <strong>multicollinearity</strong>.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<section id="closed-form-solution" class="level2">
<h2 class="anchored" data-anchor-id="closed-form-solution">2.3. Closed-form solution</h2>
<p>Ridge has a neat closed-form estimator:</p>
<p><span class="math display">\[
\hat{\beta}^{\text{ridge}} = (X^\top X + \lambda I_p)^{-1} X^\top y,
\]</span></p>
<p>where <span class="math inline">\(I_p\)</span> is the <span class="math inline">\(p \times p\)</span> identity matrix.</p>
<p>Compared to OLS:</p>
<ul>
<li>OLS: <span class="math inline">\((X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y\)</span>,</li>
<li>Ridge: adds <span class="math inline">\(\lambda I_p\)</span> to <span class="math inline">\(X^\top X\)</span> to ensure invertibility and shrinkage.</li>
</ul>
<p>This is especially helpful when:</p>
<ul>
<li><span class="math inline">\(p\)</span> is large relative to <span class="math inline">\(n\)</span>,</li>
<li><span class="math inline">\(X^\top X\)</span> is nearly singular due to strong collinearity.</li>
</ul>

</section>

<section id="strengths-of-ridge-regression" class="level1">
<h1>4. Strengths of ridge regression</h1>
<ol type="1">
<li><p><strong>Tames multicollinearity</strong><br>
Ridge is very helpful when predictors are highly correlated (common in HEOR datasets with overlapping measures of risk, utilization, and socio-demographics).</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Improves prediction stability</strong><br>
By shrinking coefficients, ridge can reduce variance and improve out-of-sample performance, especially when <span class="math inline">\(p\)</span> is large relative to <span class="math inline">\(n\)</span>.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Simple and fast to compute</strong><br>
Efficient algorithms exist (<code>glmnet</code>, etc.), and the mathematics is clean. It's easy to integrate into pipelines and cross-validation schemes.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Plays nicely with many ML ideas</strong><br>
Ridge appears in many guises (e.g., weight decay in neural nets) and provides a natural bridge from classical regression to regularized ML.</p></li>
</ol>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="limitations-of-ridge-regression" class="level1">
<h1>5. Limitations of ridge regression</h1>
<ol type="1">
<li><p><strong>No variable selection</strong><br>
Ridge shrinks coefficients, but almost never to exactly zero. If you want a sparse model (few non-zero coefficients), ridge is not ideal.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Choice of lambda is crucial</strong><br>
Too small → little regularization; too large → underfitting. While cross-validation helps, it adds complexity and computational cost.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Interpretation changes</strong><br>
Coefficients are shrunk and depend on the scale of predictors. Interpretability is trickier if variables aren't standardized and if <span class="math inline">\(\lambda\)</span> is large.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Not magic for bad data</strong><br>
Ridge helps with collinearity and overfitting, but won't fix measurement error, omitted-variable bias, or poor causal identification.</p></li>
</ol>
<hr>
</section>
<section id="why-ridge-regression-matters-for-heor-and-health-policy" class="level1">
<h1>6. Why ridge regression matters for HEOR and health policy</h1>
<p>Ridge regression is particularly useful when:</p>
<ul>
<li>You have many correlated predictors:
<ul>
<li>Comorbidity indicators,</li>
<li>Utilization measures,</li>
<li>Socioeconomic variables,</li>
<li>Prescription histories.</li>
</ul></li>
<li>You care about <strong>predicting costs, utilization, or risk</strong> accurately more than about pinning down one "true" causal effect.</li>
</ul>
<p>Use cases include:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><p><strong>Risk adjustment models</strong><br>
Predicting expected costs or utilization to adjust reimbursements or compare providers. Ridge can stabilize estimates with many overlapping risk factors.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Prognostic risk scores</strong><br>
Estimating hospitalization risk, mortality risk, or disease progression risk using a large pool of covariates.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Feature-rich observational datasets</strong><br>
Large claims or EHR datasets often have many correlated codes and measures. Ridge helps control overfitting while using many predictors.</p></li>
<li><p><strong>Input to downstream decision models</strong><br>
Predictions from ridge models (e.g., expected costs, risks) can feed into cost-effectiveness models, budget impact analysis, or simulations.</p></li>
</ol>
