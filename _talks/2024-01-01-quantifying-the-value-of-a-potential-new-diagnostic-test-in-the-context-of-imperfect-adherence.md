## Abstract

**Purpose**

To develop a framework for determining the minimum operating criteria of a new diagnostic test when the current test has imperfect adherence but has better sensitivity and specificity.

**Methods**

We used a decision tree to compare the net monetary benefit (NMB) of one-time testing with the current test (specificity=0.87, sensitivity=0.91, adherence=60%) and the introduction of a new test (with predefined specificity=0.8, sensitivity=0.70, adherence=70%). While keeping 2 out of 3 of the new test predefined characteristics constant, we varied the sensitivity, specificity, and adherence of the new test and identified parameter sets that resulted in the same NMB as a reference case. We examined three scenarios for the adoption of a new test in a population: 1) the new test replaces the current test and becomes the new standard; 2) the new test is used only among those who do not adhere to the standard; and 3) the new test leads some people to switch from the standard to the new test. We compared both tests’ NMB in different reference cases. In scenario 1, the reference case is the current test with 60% adherence. In scenario 2, the reference case is the current test with 80% adherence. Scenario 3 involves applying the current test to 60% of the population and the new test to 30%.

**Results**

In scenario 1, at least one of the new test predefined characteristics must change , i.e. the test must have a specificity of 0.88, sensitivity of 0.77, or adherence of 84% to achieve the same NMB as the reference case. For scenario 2, complementation allows the new test to have one weaker characteristic than its predefined characteristics, i.e., specificity could be as low as 0.60, sensitivity as low as 0.52, or adherence as low as 22%. For scenario 3, the new test must have a specificity of at least 0.90 or sensitivity of 0.78 to achieve the same NMB when 40% of the current test users switch to test B. **Conclusion**

Quantifying the value of a new diagnostic test should incorporate the adherence of the new and current tests and potential substitutions between tests. This is especially true when the new test has better adherence but poorer sensitivity or specificity. Knowing the lower bounds of the new test characteristics is important to avoid loss of benefits when evaluating new diagnostic technology.
