## Abstract

**Introduction**: Health systems fragmentation (HSF) can lead to inefficiencies, inequities, and suboptimal health outcomes. This presentation analyzes the temporal and territorial evolution of maternal mortality in Mexico and its potential relationship with HSF.

**Methods**: An ecological-longitudinal study analyzed maternal mortality ratios (MMR) in Mexico from 2000 to 2022 using official mortality records. The study estimated annual MMRs at national and state levels by health insurance status and compared demographic and place-of-residence characteristics among women with and without social security.

**Results**: Mexico's MMR declined between 2000 and 2018, then increased again after 2019. Most maternal deaths historically occurred among women without social security, but the relative distribution shifted over time. Important disparities were observed by schooling, marital status, rural residence, indigenous presence, and social marginalization, and recent years showed a transition toward higher MMR ratios among the population with social security.

**Conclusion**: Monitoring maternal mortality gaps by social security status can help evaluate performance in a fragmented health system, identify inequalities in access to maternal services, and inform policies to reduce disparities.
